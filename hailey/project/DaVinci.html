Leonardo da Vinci

Who the hell is he?

Leonardo da Vinci was a painter in the Renaissance, most famous for his paintings of The Mona Lisa and The Last Supper. He was not only a painter but a scientist, engineer, theorist, sculptor, and architect. He was also known for his notebooks, which contained drawings of a variety of subjects including, human anatomy, astronomy, botany, cartography, painting, and palaeontology (study of fossils). Leonardo was widely regarded as a genius of his time, conceptualising flying machines and military devices, showing that he was far ahead of his time.

Leonardo da Vinci was born 15th of April, 1452, in Anchino and died 2nd of May, 1519, in Cloux, at the age of 67. He was an illegitimate child, born out of wedlock to Piero da Vinci and Caterina, a peasant woman. Since birth, his artistic talents were recognised, which led to his family focusing on developing his artistic skills. Around the age of 14, he became a garzone (studio boy) in the workshop of Andrea del Verrocchio, who was a leading Florentine painter and sculptor of his time. Leonardo became an apprentice at the age of 17 and trained for the next 7 years.

In 1472, Leonardo was accepted into the painters' guild of Florence, but he remained in his teacher’s workshop for five more years, and after that, worked independently in Florence until 1481.

First Milanese period (c. 1482–1499)

He moved to Milan in 1482, seeking the patronage of Ludovico Sforza, the Duke of Milan. Leonardo sought a position that not only allowed him to work as an artist but also as a military engineer and architect. When he arrived, however, his first project was a commission for a painting, which is now known as “The Virgin of the Rocks.” He then moved on to work on an equestrian monument (the Sforza horse statue), designing war machines (such as armoured vehicles and giant crossbows), elaborate court amusements, and envisioning flying machines. His last masterpiece was “The Last Supper”, a painting which showcases the betrayal of Jesus. Due to the French invasion in 1499 and the fall of his patron, Duke Ludovico Sforza, he was forced to flee to Venice and then Florence.

Second Florentine period (1500–1508)

He left the city with the companionship of mathematician Luca Pacioli, and after being long absent, he was “received with acclaim and honored as a renowned native son.” During that time, he seemed to focus more on mathematics, particularly geometry, than on painting. Leonardo left Florence in the summer of 1502 to become a “senior military architect and general engineer.” He created a plan to build a canal that would connect Florence by water with the sea, which was extremely advanced in the 13th century. He produced a map, but unfortunately, the project was never carried out. Centuries later, the express highway from Florence was constructed over the exact route that Leonardo chose for his canal.

Between 1500 and 1506, he began his three great works that increased his fame even further: The Virgin, the Child Jesus, and Saint Anne (1503-19), Mona Lisa (1503-19), and Battle of Anghiari (1503-unfinished). As we all know, the Mona Lisa, now located in the Louvre Museum in Paris, France, is a widely famous painting that many flock to get a glimpse at. There are many debates and controversies as to why exactly she is so popular, such as her enchanting smile, her gaze, the skillful art techniques, and so on. Even back when Leonardo painted the portrait, it was received with praise amongst the Florentines for capturing the essence of a human face.

Second Milanese period (1503-13)

In 1506, Charles d’Amboise, the French governor in Milan, asked the Signoria (the lord) in Florence if Leonardo could travel to Milan, resulting in the Battle of Anghiari staying unfinished. Leonardo’s scientific activity increased during this period in Milan. His study of anatomy created exact and detailed reproductions of the human body and its organs, as well as comparative anatomy and physiology. However, in terms of art, he did very little as a painter, focusing more on his other interests.

Roman and French period (1513-16)

The expulsion of the French from Milan caused Leonardo to move again, this time to Rome. The brother of the new pope, Leo X, received him, and Leonardo hoped to find employment through him. However, he was unsuccessful in gaining the favour of the Pope. Leonardo also became ill during this time, which may have been his first multiple strokes that eventually led to his death.

At the age of 65, Leonardo accepted the invitation of the young King Francis I to enter his service in France. He spent the last three years of his life in the small residence of Cloux, near the king’s summer palace. The king treated him as an honoured guest, allowing him the freedom of action. He died at the age of 67, possibly due to a stroke.

Painting techniques

His art style is defined by scientific observation, deep humanism, and techniques like sfumato (“smoked off”, i.e., “blurred”), a painting technique for softening the transition between colours and tones, mimicking an area beyond what the human eye focuses on. It was used for a highly illusionistic rendering of facial features and for atmospheric effects. Leonardo also used another technique called chiaroscuro (from Italian chiaro, “light”, and scuro, “dark”). It is a visual art technique using strong contrasts between light and dark to define three-dimensional objects, creating form and volume while drawing focus.
